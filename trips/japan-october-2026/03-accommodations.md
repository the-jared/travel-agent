# Accommodations Guide — Japan October 2026

## Neighborhood Guide for Foodies

### Tokyo

**Shinjuku** — The all-rounder. Omoide Yokocho (Memory Lane) has dozens of tiny yakitori and ramen stalls steps from the station. Late-night izakayas line every alley. Excellent transit hub for day trips.

**Nihonbashi** — Old-money Tokyo. Home to the original Ningyocho sweet shops, tempura legends like Tsunahachi, and the sprawling basement food halls of Takashimaya and Coredo Muromachi. Quieter at night, but walkable to Ginza and Tsukiji Outer Market.

### Kyoto

**Kawaramachi / Shijo** — The epicenter. Nishiki Market ("Kyoto's Kitchen") is one block north. Pontocho alley runs parallel to the Kamo River with kaiseki restaurants, riverside yuka dining in October's last warm evenings, and standing bars tucked behind noren curtains. Walk to Gion in 10 minutes.

### Osaka

**Namba / Dotonbori** — Japan's street-food capital. Takoyaki (octopus balls) at Wanaka, kushikatsu at Daruma, okonomiyaki at Mizuno — all within a 5-minute walk. Kuromon Market is a 10-minute walk south. Loud, neon-lit, and open very late.

## Ryokan Guide

A ryokan (traditional Japanese inn) is a must-do at least one night. What to expect:

- **Tatami rooms** — You sleep on a futon laid out on tatami mats. Staff set it up while you're at dinner.
- **Onsen** — Most ryokans have communal hot-spring baths (segregated by gender) and some offer private baths (kashikiri buro).
- **Kaiseki dinner** — Multi-course seasonal Japanese cuisine served in your room or a private dining area. October means matsutake mushroom, persimmon, and sanma (Pacific saury).
- **Yukata** — A cotton robe provided for wearing around the property and to meals.
- **Etiquette** — Shoes off at the entrance. Wash thoroughly before entering the bath. Tattoo policies vary (ask ahead or book a private bath).

## Capsule Hotel Guide

Capsule hotels are a uniquely Japanese experience worth trying for at least one night.

- **The pod** — Roughly 2m x 1m x 1.25m. Contains a mattress, light, power outlet, small TV, and a curtain or shutter for privacy.
- **Amenities** — Shared bathrooms, lockers, vending machines. Many include a sauna or small bath.
- **Luggage** — Large suitcases generally cannot fit inside the pod. Use a coin locker or forward luggage via Takkyubin.
- **Gender** — Many capsule hotels are male-only. Mixed-gender options: Nine Hours (all locations), First Cabin, Capsule by Container Hotel.
- **Noise** — Bring earplugs. Snoring travels.
- **Cost** — $30-60/night. The experience, not the savings, is the point.

### Booking Tips

- **Book 3-4 months ahead** for October (autumn leaf season begins late October — high demand in Kyoto).
- **Booking.com** and **Agoda** have the widest Japan inventory.
- **Rakuten Travel** and **Jalan.net** (Japanese OTAs) sometimes have exclusive ryokan rates — use Google Translate.
- **Cancel-free bookings** — Book refundable rates early, then rebook cheaper closer to the date if prices drop.
- **Ryokans** — Book directly on the ryokan website or via **Relux** (Japanese luxury booking platform) for the best rates.
