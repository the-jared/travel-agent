## Cultural Quick Reference — The Big 3 Rules

1. **Shoes off** — Remove shoes when entering homes, ryokans, some restaurants, and temples. Look for a raised floor or shoe rack at the entrance. Slippers are usually provided.

2. **Quiet in public transit** — Trains and buses are silent zones. Phone calls are a no-go. Set your phone to silent (マナーモード). Speak softly if you must.

3. **No tipping, ever** — Tipping is not customary and can cause confusion or offense. The price on the menu is what you pay. Service is already exceptional.

## Money Quick Reference

- **Currency:** Japanese Yen (¥ / JPY)
- **No tipping** — never, anywhere
- **Cash is still important** — many small restaurants and shops are cash-only
- **Best ATMs:** 7-Eleven (7-Bank) and Japan Post ATMs accept international cards 24/7
- **IC Card (Suica/Pasmo):** Rechargeable transit card that also works at convenience stores and vending machines
- **Budget per day:** ~$150-250/day (mid-range, including accommodation)
- **Approximate costs:** Bowl of ramen ¥900-1,200 (~$6-8), convenience store meal ¥500-800 (~$3-5), temple entry ¥300-600 (~$2-4)

## What to Do If Something Goes Wrong

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| **Lost passport** | Go to US Embassy/Consulate immediately with a photo ID copy |
| **Medical emergency** | Call 119, go to nearest hospital; Tourist Hotline can help with translation |
| **Lost/stolen item on train** | Go to the station's Lost & Found (忘れ物センター); Japanese return rate is remarkably high |
| **Need English help** | Call Tourist Hotline 050-3816-2787 (24/7, multilingual) |
| **Natural disaster** | Follow NHK World alerts, shelter in place; download the Safety Tips app |
