## Emergency Numbers

| Service | Number | Notes |
|---------|--------|-------|
| **Police** | **110** | English support available (request it) |
| **Ambulance / Fire** | **119** | Say "kyuukyuu" (ambulance) or "kaji" (fire) |
| **Japan Tourist Hotline** | **050-3816-2787** | 24/7, English/Chinese/Korean. Travel help, lost items, emergencies. YOUR BEST FRIEND. |
| **US Embassy Tokyo** | 03-3224-5000 | Akasaka, Minato-ku. For passport loss, legal trouble. |
| **Emergency from mobile** | 110 or 119 | Works without SIM. Provide location in English; they will assist. |
| **Lost phone / item on train** | Ask station staff | JR Lost & Found: 050-2016-1601. Items are almost always recovered in Japan. |

## Top 10 Survival Phrases

| English | Japanese (Romanized) | Japanese | When to Use |
|---------|---------------------|----------|-------------|
| Thank you | **Arigatou gozaimasu** | ありがとうございます | Constantly. After every interaction. |
| Excuse me / Sorry | **Sumimasen** | すみません | Getting attention, apologizing, passing through crowds |
| Please (when asking) | **Onegaishimasu** | お願いします | After pointing at a menu item, requesting something |
| Yes / No | **Hai / Iie** | はい / いいえ | Answering questions |
| I don't understand | **Wakarimasen** | わかりません | When someone speaks rapid Japanese to you |
| Do you speak English? | **Eigo wa hanasemasu ka?** | 英語は話せますか？ | Try this before giving up on communication |
| Where is ___? | **___ wa doko desu ka?** | ___はどこですか？ | Finding stations, bathrooms, restaurants |
| How much? | **Ikura desu ka?** | いくらですか？ | Shopping, street food stalls |
| This one, please | **Kore o kudasai** | これをください | Pointing at food, menu items, products |
| Check, please | **Okaikei onegaishimasu** | お会計お願いします | Restaurants — or just gesture "check please" |

**Bonus phrases:**
- "Oishii!" (おいしい) = Delicious! — Say this to any chef and watch them light up.
- "Kanpai!" (乾杯) = Cheers! — Essential for izakaya drinking.
- "Toire wa doko desu ka?" (トイレはどこですか？) = Where is the toilet?

## Hotel Address in Japanese (For Taxis)

Show this to your taxi driver. Fill in your current hotel before going out:

```
ホテル名: ___________________________
(Hotel Name)

住所: ___________________________
(Address)

電話番号: ___________________________
(Phone Number)
```

**Tip:** Screenshot your hotel's name and address in Japanese from Google Maps. Taxi drivers may not read English addresses but will always recognize Japanese text or a map pin.

**Taxi basics:**
- Doors open and close AUTOMATICALLY. Do not touch the door.
- No tipping. Ever.
- Payment: Cash preferred. Major taxis accept IC cards and credit cards.
- Say your destination or show the address. "_____ made onegaishimasu" = "To _____, please."

## Tipping Rule

### NEVER TIP. ANYWHERE. EVER.

- Not at restaurants. Not at hotels. Not in taxis. Not at bars.
- Tipping can be considered **confusing or even rude**. Staff may chase you down to return the money thinking you forgot it.
- Service charges (10-15%) are included at upscale restaurants automatically.
- If you want to show gratitude, say "Arigatou gozaimasu" with a slight bow. That means more than money.

## Key Cultural Rules

### Shoes
- **Remove shoes** when entering: homes, ryokans, some restaurants (look for a genkan/entryway with shoe shelves), temple interiors, fitting rooms.
- Wear provided slippers indoors. Switch to separate toilet slippers in the bathroom (and switch BACK when you leave — the #1 tourist mistake).

### Trains
- **No talking on the phone.** Set to silent mode (called "manner mode" in Japan).
- **No eating on local trains.** Shinkansen is fine — bento boxes are part of the experience.
- **Stand on the correct side of escalators:** Left in Tokyo, Right in Osaka/Kyoto.
- **Queue in marked lines** on the platform. Let passengers off before boarding.

### Eating
- **Say "itadakimasu"** (いただきます) before eating — means "I humbly receive."
- **Slurp your noodles.** It's polite and shows enjoyment. Seriously — slurp loudly at ramen shops.
- **Don't stick chopsticks upright in rice.** It resembles a funeral ritual.
- **Don't pass food chopstick-to-chopstick.** Also a funeral association.
- **Don't pour your own drink** in a group setting. Pour for others; they'll pour for you.

### Onsen (Hot Spring Baths)
- **Wash thoroughly** at the shower stations BEFORE entering the bath.
- **No swimsuits.** Everyone is naked. It's normal and no one is looking at you.
- **Tattoos:** Some onsen ban tattoos. Options: ask beforehand, use cover-up bandages, or book a private bath (kashikiri buro).
- **Bring a small towel** to the bathing area (provided). Do NOT put it in the water — fold it on your head.

### General Manners
- **Don't blow your nose in public.** Sniffling is acceptable; honking into a tissue is not.
- **Bow slightly** when greeting, thanking, or saying goodbye. A small nod is fine for tourists.
- **Don't eat while walking.** Find a bench or stand near the food stall. Exception: festival/market settings.
- **Trash cans are rare.** Carry a small bag for your garbage. Dispose at konbini or your hotel.

## Transit Quick Tips

### Last Trains

| City | Approximate Last Train | Notes |
|------|----------------------|-------|
| **Tokyo** | ~12:00-12:30 AM | Yamanote Line last train varies by direction. Check Navitime app. |
| **Kyoto** | ~11:30 PM-12:00 AM | Buses stop earlier (~11 PM). Subway runs slightly later. |
| **Osaka** | ~12:00-12:20 AM | Midosuji Line runs latest. |

**If you miss the last train:**
1. Taxi (expensive: ¥3,000-10,000+ depending on distance)
2. Karaoke until the first train (~5:00 AM) — the classic Japanese move
3. Manga cafe (Manga Kissa) — reclinable seats, drinks, showers. ~¥1,500-2,500 for the night.
4. 24-hour McDonald's — the budget last resort

### Suica / IC Card Tips
- Tap in, tap out. If you tap in and don't tap out, the card locks. Ask station staff to fix it.
- Recharge at any ticket machine (look for IC card / Suica buttons). Accepts cash and sometimes credit cards.
- Works at konbini (7-Eleven, Lawson, FamilyMart), vending machines, and many restaurants.
- Balance limit: ¥20,000.

### Shinkansen Tips
- **Reserved seats:** Use your JR Pass at a ticket machine or counter to reserve seats for free. Recommended for popular routes.
- **Unreserved cars:** Cars 1-3 on most Hikari/Kodama trains. Queue early at the platform for busy departures.
- **Luggage:** Oversized bags (3 sides totaling 160+ cm) require a free reservation for the luggage storage area behind the last row. Book at the ticket counter or online.
- **Food:** Buy an ekiben (station bento) and drinks before boarding. There is no food cart on most services anymore.

## WiFi Backup

If your eSIM fails:
- **Free WiFi** at all konbini (7-Eleven, Lawson, FamilyMart) — connect to their network, accept terms.
- **Japan Connected-free Wi-Fi app** — One registration, access to thousands of free hotspots.
- **Starbucks / McDonald's** — Reliable free WiFi.
- **Train stations** — JR stations and Tokyo Metro stations have free WiFi (often slow).
- **Pocket WiFi rental** — Available at airports (Global WiFi, iVideo). ~$5-8/day.
