Entry Requirements

Visa

  • for tourism.
  • No pre-registration or visa application needed.
  • You receive a "temporary visitor" stamp on arrival.
  • You may be asked to show proof of a return/onward ticket and sufficient funds (rarely enforced, but have documentation accessible).

    • Passport

  • (Japan does not require 6 months validity, but having extra validity is always wise).
  • Ensure at least 2 blank pages for entry/exit stamps.

    • Arrival Process

  • **Visit Japan Web**: Complete the online entry form at [Visit Japan Web](https://www.vjw.digital.go.jp/) before departure. This covers immigration, customs, and tax-free purchase registration. Complete it at least 24 hours before landing.
  • **Immigration**: Show passport + QR code from Visit Japan Web. Fingerprinting and photo are taken at the immigration counter for all foreign nationals.
  • **Customs**: Declare if carrying over ¥1,000,000 in cash, or restricted items. Standard duty-free allowances: 3 bottles of alcohol (760ml each), 400 cigarettes, 2 oz perfume.
  • **Prohibited items**: No recreational drugs (zero tolerance policy — even CBD is illegal). Certain cold medicines containing pseudoephedrine (like Sudafed) require advance import permission. Stimulant drugs (Adderall, etc.) are **strictly prohibited**.

    • Japan Travel Authorization (JETA)

    As of 2025, Japan is phasing in an electronic travel authorization system similar to ESTA. Check the Japanese Ministry of Foreign Affairs website 60 days before travel to confirm whether JETA is required for your trip.

    • Tap Water

  • in Japan. Tap water quality is excellent. Vending machines and convenience stores also sell water for ¥100-150.

    • Pharmacies & Medical Care

  • Over-the-counter medication is available but formulations differ from the US. Japanese cold medicine (like Pabron) is effective. Bring any prescription medications in original containers with a doctor's note.
  • **Hospitals**: Japan has world-class medical care. English-speaking clinics exist in major cities (AMDA International Medical Information Center: 03-5285-8088). Emergency room visits without insurance can cost ¥20,000-50,000+.

    • Safety

  • for travelers. Violent crime against tourists is extremely rare. The US State Department rates Japan as **Level 1 — Exercise Normal Precautions**.
  • Solo travelers, including solo women, generally feel very safe walking at night in most areas.
  • Lost items are frequently turned in. Check at the nearest koban (police box) or station lost-and-found.

    • Natural Disaster Risks (October)

  • technically runs through October, though it winds down. Late-season typhoons are possible but less frequent. Monitor the Japan Meteorological Agency (JMA) website and have the NHK World app for alerts.
  • **Earthquakes**: Japan experiences frequent small earthquakes. Know the basics: drop, cover, hold on. Your hotel will have evacuation instructions. The earthquake early warning system sends alerts to phones.
  • **Tsunami**: Relevant only for coastal areas after a major quake. Follow evacuation signs (blue signs with a wave symbol pointing to higher ground).

    • Common Scams & Annoyances

  • If it sounds too good to be true, it is.
  • **Fake monks**: People in monk robes may approach you for "donations" near tourist spots. Real monks do not solicit on the street.
  • **Overcharging at unlicensed cabs**: Use only official taxis (green license plates) or ride-hailing apps.
  • **Drink spiking**: Rare but possible in Roppongi nightlife. Standard precautions apply.

    • Tipping

    **Do not tip in Japan.** Tipping is not part of Japanese culture and can cause confusion or even offense. This applies to restaurants, taxis, hotels, and tour guides. Exceptional service is the baseline standard, not an extra.

    • Cultural Do's and Don'ts

    Japan has deep, layered social customs. You are not expected to know all of them, and Japanese people are gracious with foreigners. But making the effort shows respect and enriches your experience enormously.

    Do

  • **Bow when greeting and thanking**. A slight bow (15-30 degrees) is appropriate for most interactions. Deeper bows show more respect. You do not need to bow to shop clerks (a nod is fine), but do bow when meeting someone, receiving a gift, or at temples/shrines.
  • **Say "itadakimasu" before meals and "gochisousama deshita" after**. This is not a prayer — it is an expression of gratitude for the food, the cook, and the ingredients. Skipping it is noticed.
  • **Remove your shoes when entering homes, ryokans, some restaurants, and temple interiors**. Look for a genkan (entryway step) and shoe shelves. Slippers are usually provided. Remove slippers before stepping on tatami mats. There are separate toilet slippers in bathrooms — do not forget to switch back.
  • **Carry a small towel (tenugui) or handkerchief**. Many public restrooms lack paper towels or hand dryers. A small towel is standard in Japan.
  • **Queue patiently and in orderly lines**. Japanese queuing culture is precise. At train platforms, stand behind the marked lines and wait for passengers to exit before boarding. Stand on the left side of escalators in Tokyo (right side in Osaka) to let people pass.
  • **Accept and give items with both hands**. When receiving a business card, change, or gift, use both hands. This shows respect and attentiveness.
  • **Wash thoroughly before entering an onsen (hot spring)**. The bathing area has shower stations — sit on the stool, scrub yourself completely, rinse off all soap, then enter the communal bath. The bath is for soaking, not washing.
  • **Dispose of trash properly or carry it with you**. Public trash cans are rare. Carry a small bag for your trash. When you do find bins, they are sorted: burnables, plastics, PET bottles, cans, glass. Follow the sorting system.
  • **Use the tray for cash transactions**. Many shops have a small tray at the register. Place your cash/card on the tray rather than handing it directly.
  • **Be punctual**. If a train departs at 10:32, it departs at 10:32. If you have a dinner reservation at 7:00, arrive at 6:55. Lateness is disrespectful.
  • **Learn to use chopsticks properly before the trip**. Practice at home. It matters.
  • **Return your tray at fast-food and self-service restaurants**. Clear your table, sort the garbage, and return the tray to the designated spot.

    • Don't

  • **Do not tip**. Not at restaurants, not in taxis, not at hotels. It can cause genuine confusion. (See Money section above.)
  • **Do not eat or drink while walking**. It is considered inconsiderate. The exception is at festival stalls (matsuri) and designated eating areas near food vendors. If you buy a drink from a vending machine, stand near it and finish before walking.
  • **Do not talk on your phone on trains or buses**. Set your phone to silent (manner mode) on public transit. Texting is fine; speaking is not. Near priority seats, you should also avoid using your phone entirely.
  • **Do not blow your nose in public**. Sniffling is acceptable (even common); blowing your nose loudly is considered disgusting. Step into a restroom if you need to blow your nose.
  • **Do not stick chopsticks upright in rice**. This resembles a funeral ritual (incense sticks in offering bowls) and is deeply taboo. Also do not pass food chopstick-to-chopstick, as this mimics a funeral bone-gathering ceremony.
  • **Do not point at people or things with chopsticks**. Use them only for eating.
  • **Do not enter an onsen with tattoos visible** (at most traditional establishments). Tattoos are associated with yakuza in Japan. Some onsens are becoming tattoo-friendly, and cover-up stickers/bandages are sometimes accepted. Research in advance or ask. Private onsen rooms (kashikiri) are always an option.
  • **Do not litter or smoke outside designated areas**. Smoking on the street is illegal in many wards of Tokyo and Osaka. Use designated smoking areas (kitsuen-jo). Fines can be ¥2,000-5,000.
  • **Do not cut in line. Ever.** Japanese queuing is sacred. Cutting will draw visible disapproval.
  • **Do not be loud in public spaces**. Japan values quietness and consideration. Keep your voice low on trains, in restaurants, and in residential areas, especially at night.
  • **Do not touch the merchandise excessively** in shops without intent to buy, especially food items.
  • **Do not jaywalk**. Even if there are no cars, wait for the signal. Japanese pedestrians follow traffic signals religiously.

    Onsen Etiquette (Step by Step)

  • Pay and enter the correct gender door (男 = men, 女 = women). Check colors — blue is typically men, red/pink is women, but read the kanji.
  • In the changing room (datsuijo), undress completely. Place clothes in a basket or locker. Take only your small towel in.
  • At the washing station, sit on the stool. Wash and rinse your entire body thoroughly with soap and shampoo. Rinse completely.
  • Enter the bath slowly. Do not put your towel in the water — fold it on your head or set it at the pool's edge.
  • Soak quietly. Do not swim, splash, or dunk your head.
  • When done, do not drain the bath. Dry off lightly before returning to the changing room.
  • No swimsuits. Onsens are nude bathing only (segregated by gender).

    • Temple & Shrine Visiting

    At a Shinto Shrine (Jinja):

  • Bow slightly before passing through the torii gate.
  • Walk to the sides of the path — the center (sei-chu) is for the deity.
  • At the temizuya (water basin): take the ladle with your right hand, pour water over your left hand, switch, pour over your right hand, then pour water into your cupped left hand to rinse your mouth (spit to the side, not back in). Let remaining water run down the handle.
  • At the main hall: throw a coin (¥5 coins are considered lucky — "go-en" sounds like "good fortune/connection"), bow twice deeply, clap twice, make your wish/prayer, bow once more.

    • At a Buddhist Temple (Tera/Ji):

  • Bow at the main gate.
  • If there is incense (osenko), buy a bundle, light it, fan out the flame (do not blow), and place it in the incense burner. Waft smoke toward you (it is believed to heal).
  • At the main hall: toss a coin, bow once, put your hands together in prayer (do not clap — that is for shrines only), bow again.

    Autumn Foliage (Koyo)

    October is the very beginning of autumn foliage season. Here is what to expect:

  • (Northern Alps near Kamikochi/Tateyama, northern Tohoku). Tokyo and Kyoto are still green.
  • **Mid-October**: Colors descend to mid-altitude areas. Nikko begins to peak. Higher elevations around Hakone show color.
  • **Late October**: Early color appears in Kyoto's mountain temples (Kurama, Takao). Tokyo parks start turning. Nikko is at or just past peak.
  • **November**: Peak foliage for Tokyo and Kyoto (typically mid-to-late November).

    • For the best foliage during your trip, focus on higher elevation and northern destinations in early-to-mid October.

    • Transit System

    Japan's public transit is the best in the world. Here is what you need to know:

    JR Pass (Japan Rail Pass):

  • Covers all JR trains nationwide, including most Shinkansen (bullet trains), JR local/express lines, some JR buses, and the JR ferry to Miyajima.
  • **Does NOT cover**: Nozomi and Mizuho Shinkansen (fastest trains on the Tokaido/Sanyo lines). Use Hikari or Sakura instead — only 10-20 minutes slower.
  • **14-day Ordinary Pass**: ~¥50,000 ($333). Worth it if you are doing Tokyo-Kyoto-Osaka-Hiroshima and other long-haul routes.
  • Purchase online at [japanrailpass.net](https://www.japanrailpass.net/) before departure and activate at a JR ticket office in Japan.
  • Reserve seats for Shinkansen for free at any JR ticket office or through the SmartEX/Japan Rail Pass app.

    • IC Cards (Suica / Pasmo / ICOCA):

  • Rechargeable transit cards that work on virtually all trains, buses, subways, convenience stores, and vending machines nationwide.
  • with Japan region or a non-Japan iPhone running iOS 16+). Android users can get Mobile Suica via Google Pay.
  • Physical IC cards are limited in availability — digital is the way to go.
  • Load at any train station machine, convenience store, or directly in the app.
  • Tap in and out at ticket gates. Fares are calculated automatically.

    • Metro/Subway:

  • Tokyo has two subway systems (Tokyo Metro and Toei) plus JR lines and private railways. They are all connected but run by different companies. IC cards work on all of them.
  • Kyoto: Bus system is more practical than the limited subway for most sightseeing. Consider a Kyoto Bus 1-Day Pass (¥700).

    • Ride-Hailing

  • **GO (タクシーGO)**: The dominant taxi-hailing app in Japan. Works like Uber. Available in major cities.
  • **Uber Japan**: Limited availability. Functions as a taxi dispatcher, not a rideshare service. Available mainly in Tokyo.
  • **Taxis**: Metered, safe, and doors open/close automatically (do not touch the door). Starting fare: ~¥500-710. A 10-minute ride costs roughly ¥1,500-2,500.

    • Public Toilets

    Japan has the best public toilets in the world.

  • Found in every train station, convenience store, department store, and public park.

    Final Tips for the Solo Foodie Traveler

  • **Counter seating is your friend**. Solo dining is completely normal in Japan — more so than almost anywhere else. Ramen shops, sushi bars, izakaya counters, and yakitori joints are designed for solo diners. Sit at the counter, watch the chef, and enjoy.
  • **Eat at odd hours**. Popular ramen shops and restaurants can have long lines at peak times. Eat lunch at 11 AM or 2 PM, dinner at 5:30 PM or 9 PM.
  • **Ticket machine restaurants (shokkenki)**: Many ramen and gyudon shops have vending machines at the entrance. Insert money, press the button for your dish, hand the ticket to the staff. No Japanese needed.
