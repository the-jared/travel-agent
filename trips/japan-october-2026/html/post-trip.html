Post-Trip Guide — Japan October 2026

Everything you need to handle in the first week after returning home.

1. Jet Lag Recovery (Eastbound US Return)

Returning from Japan to the US is eastbound travel, which is harder on the body than westbound. Japan is 13-17 hours ahead of US time zones, effectively meaning your body thinks it's tomorrow.

Recovery Protocol

Rule of thumb: ~1.5 days of adjustment per timezone crossed. For a 13-hour difference (US East Coast), expect 7-10 days for full recovery. For 16-hour difference (US West Coast), expect 10-14 days.

Day 1-2 (arrival day + next day):

  • Get morning sunlight immediately (within 30 min of waking). Walk outside for 20-30 min. This is the single most powerful reset signal.
  • Do NOT nap, no matter how tired you are. If you absolutely must, limit to 20 min before 2:00 PM.
  • Eat meals at local mealtimes even if not hungry. Meals are a circadian cue.
  • Take 0.5-1mg melatonin at your new local bedtime (not before 9:00 PM). Low dose is more effective than high dose.
  • Avoid caffeine after 12:00 PM.
  • Go to bed at your normal local bedtime (not earlier, even if exhausted).

Day 3-5:

  • Continue morning light exposure (30 min outdoor walk).
  • Continue melatonin at bedtime.
  • You'll likely wake at 3:00-5:00 AM. Don't fight it too hard — get up, keep lights dim, avoid screens. Try to stay in bed until 5:30 AM minimum.
  • Light exercise (walk, yoga) in the morning. No intense workouts in the evening.

Day 6-10:

  • Waking time normalizes. Stop melatonin once you're sleeping through to 6:00 AM.
  • Energy dips in the afternoon are normal — push through without caffeine if possible.
  • Full cognitive performance returns around day 10-14.

What NOT to Do

  • Do NOT take sleeping pills (they knock you out but don't reset your clock)
  • Do NOT stay up all night to "force" adjustment
  • Do NOT wear sunglasses in the morning (you need that light)
  • Do NOT drink alcohol to help sleep (it fragments sleep architecture)

Items to Declare

  • All food items (packaged snacks are usually fine; fresh produce/meat is NOT)
  • All purchases over $800 combined value
  • Any items purchased for others / commercial purposes

Japan-Specific Customs Notes

  • Japanese whisky: 1 liter duty-free. Additional bottles taxed at ~$2.50/750ml (federal) plus state rates. Worth bringing extra if you found good bottles.
  • Green tea / matcha: Packaged tea is fine to bring back. Declare on the form.
  • Wagashi / snacks: Packaged sweets, rice crackers, Kit Kats = all fine. No fresh items.
  • Knives (Japanese kitchen knives): Legal to bring back in checked luggage. Not duty-free if over $800 total purchases.
  • Ceramics / pottery: No restrictions. Wrap well in clothes in checked bag.
  • Prescription medications: Bring original packaging and prescription documentation.

CBP Declaration Tips

  • Keep receipts for major purchases
  • Estimate prices for small items (konbini snacks, small souvenirs)
  • If over $800: you pay duty on the amount above $800 (usually 3-5% for most goods)
  • Fill out CBP Declaration Form 6059B on the plane (one per family)

  5. - etc.
  6. [ ] Star/favorite your best 50-100 photos — These are your "hero shots" for sharing
  7. [ ] Edit hero shots — Basic adjustments: exposure, contrast, color temperature. Japan photos often benefit from slightly boosted warm tones.

Week 3-4 After Return

  1. [ ] Create a shared album for travel companions
  2. [ ] Select 20-30 photos for social media / sharing (if desired)
  3. [ ] Consider a photo book — Services: Artifact Uprising, Shutterfly, Google Photos books
  4. [ ] Archive raw files — If you shot RAW, keep the originals on an external drive. They're large but future-proof.

Photo Metadata

  • Ensure location data is on (GPS tagging) so you can always find where a photo was taken
  • If your camera doesn't geotag, sync timestamps with your phone photos to approximate locations later
