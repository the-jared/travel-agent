# Photo Guide — Japan October 2026

> Best photo spots per city with optimal timing, gear tips, and composition notes. October is ideal for photography: soft autumn light, early foliage, and clear post-rain skies.

---

## Sunrise Spots (5:45-6:30 AM in late October)

### Fushimi Inari Taisha (伏見稲荷大社) — Kyoto
- **What:** Thousands of vermillion torii gates winding up a mountain
- **Optimal time:** Arrive by 5:30 AM. Gates are empty until ~7:00 AM. By 9:00 AM it's a wall of people.
- **Best shot:** Tunnel of gates on the lower path, shooting uphill with no people. The light filtering through gaps creates orange-on-orange magic.
- **Composition:** Use the curve of the tunnel as a leading line. Shoot both with and without a person for scale.
- **Gear:** Wide angle (16-24mm) for tunnel immersion; 50-85mm for compressed gate stacking.
- **Pro tip:** The less-visited paths on the backside of the mountain (descending via the alternate route) have gates with peeling paint and moss — more atmospheric, zero crowds.

### Senso-ji Temple (浅草寺) — Tokyo
- **What:** Tokyo's oldest temple with the iconic Kaminarimon gate and five-story pagoda
- **Optimal time:** Arrive 5:30 AM. The Nakamise shopping street is shuttered but the temple is open. Dawn light hits the main hall beautifully.
- **Best shot:** Kaminarimon gate with the massive red lantern, empty street behind. Pagoda reflected in puddles after rain.
- **Composition:** Shoot the gate straight-on and centered. For the pagoda, step back to the Nakamise street side and use the row of closed shops as a frame.
- **Gear:** 24-35mm for the gate; 70-200mm for compressing the pagoda against city buildings.
- **Pro tip:** Return at night (until 23:00) when the temple and pagoda are illuminated. The contrast of red temple against blue-hour sky is spectacular.

### Arashiyama Bamboo Grove (嵐山竹林) — Kyoto
- **What:** Towering bamboo forest with a winding path
- **Optimal time:** Be at the grove by 6:00 AM. By 8:00 AM tour buses arrive. Early morning mist in October is common and gorgeous.
- **Best shot:** Looking straight up through the bamboo canopy. The path curving away with a lone figure.
- **Composition:** Vertical shots looking up work best. Use a person in a kimono (rental shops open at 9:00) for a classic shot, or shoot the empty path at dawn.
- **Gear:** Ultra-wide (14-20mm) for looking up; 35-50mm for the path perspective.

## Sunset Spots (5:00-5:30 PM in late October)

### Shibuya Sky (渋谷スカイ) — Tokyo
- **What:** Open-air rooftop observation deck atop Shibuya Scramble Square, 230m high
- **Optimal time:** Book the 4:00 PM or 4:30 PM slot. You'll catch golden hour, sunset, AND the city lights turning on.
- **Best shot:** The Shibuya Crossing from directly above. The infinity edge with sunset. Tokyo Tower and Mt. Fuji silhouette on clear days.
- **Composition:** Shoot straight down at the scramble crossing. Use the edge railing for leading lines. Panoramas work incredibly well here.
- **Gear:** Wide angle essential. Phone cameras work great here (computational photography handles the dynamic range well).
- **Pro tip:** Tripods are not allowed, but you can rest your camera on the railing. The net barrier is invisible at wide apertures (shoot f/2.8 or wider pressed against the net).

### Arashiyama — Togetsukyo Bridge (渡月橋) — Kyoto
- **What:** Iconic wooden bridge spanning the Hozu River with mountains behind
- **Optimal time:** 4:30-5:15 PM. The mountains behind the bridge catch the last golden light. October foliage beginning to turn adds warm tones.
- **Best shot:** The bridge with autumn-tinted mountains. Boats on the river with bridge in background.
- **Composition:** Shoot from the south bank looking north for mountain backdrop. From upstream (west) for the classic postcard angle with the bridge and mountains.
- **Gear:** 24-70mm zoom for versatility; 70-200mm to compress the bridge against mountains.

### Osaka Umeda Sky Building (空中庭園展望台)
- **What:** Futuristic floating garden observatory connecting two towers, 173m high
- **Optimal time:** 4:30 PM entry. Sunset from the open-air rooftop level.
- **Best shot:** The dramatic escalator tunnel ascending to the observatory. Sunset over Osaka with the city grid below.
- **Composition:** Use the circular opening in the roof as a natural frame. The escalator tube makes a killer perspective shot.
- **Gear:** Wide angle for the architecture; any lens for sunset.
- **Pro tip:** Less crowded than Shibuya Sky and arguably a more interesting building to photograph.

## Night Photography

### Golden Gai (ゴールデン街) — Tokyo
- **What:** Six narrow alleys of 200+ tiny bars, each seating 5-10 people, covered in stickers and neon
- **Optimal time:** 8:00-10:00 PM. Early enough for fewer crowds, late enough for the neon to be blazing.
- **Best shot:** The narrow alleys with warm light spilling from doorways. Close-ups of hand-painted signs. A lone figure in the alley.
- **Composition:** Shoot down the length of an alley. Use the narrow walls as a natural frame. Shoot from low (knee height) for a more dramatic perspective.
- **Gear:** 35mm f/1.4 or 50mm f/1.4 — you need fast glass. ISO 1600-6400 is fine for the gritty aesthetic.
- **Pro tip:** Ask before photographing people or bar interiors. Some bars have a "no photos" policy. The alleys themselves are fair game.

### Dotonbori Neon (道頓堀) — Osaka
- **What:** Canal-side strip of massive animated neon signs, including the iconic Glico Running Man
- **Optimal time:** 7:00-9:00 PM for maximum neon. Signs are on until ~midnight.
- **Best shot:** The Glico Running Man from the Ebisu Bridge. The full canal reflection of neon signs. The crab and dragon 3D signs.
- **Composition:** From Ebisu Bridge, shoot the Glico sign centered. For reflections, get to the canal-level walkway and shoot along the water surface.
- **Gear:** 24-35mm from the bridge; 50-85mm for individual signs. Tripod for long-exposure reflections on the canal.
- **Pro tip:** The canal walkway below Ebisu Bridge is where you get the neon reflections in the water. Arrive early to claim a spot on the railing.

### Miyajima Torii Gate (厳島神社大鳥居) — Day Trip from Osaka/Hiroshima
- **What:** The "floating" torii gate in the sea (recently renovated, looks pristine)
- **Optimal time:** Check tide tables. High tide = gate appears to float. Night illumination until 23:00.
- **Best shot:** Gate at high tide with sunset behind. Night illumination with reflections.
- **Composition:** From the shore at Itsukushima Shrine for the classic straight-on shot. Walk to the left side of the island for an angle with mountains behind.
- **Gear:** 70-200mm for compression from shore; 24-70mm if you wade out at low tide.
- **Note:** This requires a day trip from Osaka (2h by shinkansen to Hiroshima + ferry) or an overnight. Plan accordingly.

## Mt. Fuji from Hakone

### Hakone Ropeway / Owakudani (大涌谷)
- **What:** Volcanic valley with sulfur vents and Mt. Fuji behind
- **Optimal time:** Early morning (before 10:00 AM) for clearest views. Fuji is often clouded by afternoon.
- **Best shot:** Fuji from the ropeway gondola. Fuji framed by sulfur steam at Owakudani. Lake Ashi with Fuji.
- **Composition:** Use the volcanic steam as foreground interest with Fuji behind. From Lake Ashi, use the red torii gate of Hakone Shrine as a frame.
- **Gear:** 70-200mm to pull Fuji close; 24-70mm for environmental shots.
- **Pro tip:** October is one of the best months for Fuji visibility. If it's clear at your hotel in the morning, go immediately — don't wait.

### Lake Ashi (芦ノ湖)
- **What:** Caldera lake with Fuji reflections on still mornings
- **Optimal time:** 6:00-8:00 AM for mirror-still water and Fuji reflections.
- **Best shot:** Fuji reflected in the lake, Hakone Shrine torii in foreground.
- **Composition:** South shore looking north. Use the pirate ship or shrine torii as foreground elements.

## October Foliage Spots

Peak foliage in 2026 is estimated:
- **Tokyo:** Late November (early color in late October in parks)
- **Kyoto:** Mid-to-late November (but Tofuku-ji and high-altitude temples start in late October)
- **Hakone:** Late October to mid-November (best timing for this trip!)

## General Photo Tips for Japan

1. **Respect temple rules** — Many interiors prohibit photography. Look for the camera-with-X signs.
2. **No tripods** in most temples and observation decks. A small gorillapod or beanbag works as an alternative.
3. **People photography** — Japan has strict privacy norms. Don't photograph people's faces close-up without permission. Street scenes from a distance are fine.
4. **Vending machine glow** — Use the omnipresent vending machines as a light source for moody night portraits on quiet streets.
5. **Rainy day = best day** — Wet streets reflect neon. Umbrellas add color to compositions. Temples in rain are atmospheric and less crowded.
6. **Convenience store portraits** — The warm light of a konbini (7-Eleven, Lawson) at night makes for iconic Japan street photos.
7. **Manhole covers** — Japan has beautifully designed manhole covers unique to each city. Shoot them when wet for best contrast.

## Phone Photography Settings

If shooting on iPhone/Pixel:
- Shoot in 48MP (iPhone) or full resolution mode
- Use 0.5x ultra-wide for temples and narrow alleys
- Use 2x or 3x for food photos (natural compression, less distortion)
- Turn on grid lines for level compositions
- Night mode auto-activates — let it work, brace against a wall for 3-second exposures
- Shoot RAW (ProRAW on iPhone) for maximum editing flexibility of neon/sunset scenes
